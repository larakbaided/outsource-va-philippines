Most business owners know they *should* delegate — they just don't know where to start. The trick is to hand off the work that drains your time without requiring your unique expertise.

Here are 15 tasks a virtual assistant can take off your plate, grouped by area. Start with the ones that eat the most of your week.

## Administrative & executive support

1. **Inbox management** — triaging, sorting, flagging, and drafting routine replies
2. **Calendar management** — scheduling, rescheduling, and protecting your focus time
3. **Meeting coordination** — booking, sending invites, preparing agendas
4. **Travel and logistics** — booking and organizing itineraries
5. **Data entry and organization** — keeping your files, sheets, and records tidy

## CRM & operations

6. **CRM cleanup** — deduping contacts, tagging, and keeping data reliable
7. **Pipeline management** — updating deal stages so nothing slips
8. **Following up with leads** — sending timely, consistent follow-ups
9. **Building workflows and automations** (in GoHighLevel or similar) so routine follow-up runs itself
10. **Reporting** — assembling weekly or monthly performance summaries

## Marketing & content

11. **Social media scheduling** — planning and publishing your content calendar
12. **Email campaigns** — building, scheduling, and testing sends
13. **Content repurposing** — turning one video or post into many
14. **Basic graphics** — simple, on-brand visuals in Canva
15. **Research** — competitors, prospects, tools, and topics

## How to decide what to delegate first

Try this quick filter. For each task, ask:

- **Does it drain me but not require my expertise?** → Delegate it first.
- **Is it repeatable?** → Great candidate; document it once, hand it off forever.
- **Only I can do this well?** → Keep it (for now).

The goal isn't to offload everything — it's to reclaim your time for the work only you can do: strategy, relationships, and growth.

## A word on doing it well

Delegation fails when it's rushed. Give your virtual assistant context, document the process (or have them document as they learn), and start with a few tasks before expanding. Done right, each task you hand off is time you get back — permanently.
